# Hydrostatic Stability

The geometry and mass-distribution models established in Chapter 2 provide the foundation for the hydrostatic stability analysis. Unlike the original IB Mathematics IA, the restoring behavior is not introduced through an assumed critical-angle formula. Instead, it is obtained from the geometry of the submerged body and the resulting buoyancy distribution.

For each tilt angle, the model first determines the floating equilibrium, then computes the submerged region, the center of buoyancy, the restoring arm, and finally the restoring moment. The critical stability condition, if it exists, is therefore determined numerically rather than assumed analytically.

---

## 3.1 Floating Equilibrium

When a swimming ring floats on the water surface, it is acted upon primarily by two external forces:

- the gravitational force (weight), and
- the buoyant force generated by the displaced water.

The weight of the swimming ring is

$$
W = 
mg,
$$

where

* $m$ is the total mass of the swimming ring, and
* $g$ is the gravitational acceleration.

According to Archimedes' Principle, the buoyant force equals the weight of the displaced water,

$$
F_B = 
\rho_{\mathrm{water}}gV_{\mathrm{sub}},
$$

where

* $\rho_{\mathrm{water}}$ is the density of water, and
* $V_{\mathrm{sub}}$ is the submerged volume.

For static floating equilibrium,

$$
F_B=W.
$$

Substituting the two expressions above gives

$$
\rho_{\mathrm{water}}gV_{\mathrm{sub}} = 
mg.
$$

After cancelling the common factor $g$, the floating equilibrium condition becomes

$$
\rho_{\mathrm{water}} \cdot V_{\mathrm{sub}} = 
m.
$$

This equation determines how much of the swimming ring must remain below the water surface. However, unlike a simple floating sphere or cylinder, the submerged volume of a tilted torus cannot generally be written as a closed-form function.

Instead, the submerged volume depends on both the tilt angle

$$
\theta
$$

and the vertical position of the swimming ring

$$
h.
$$

Therefore,

$$
V_{\mathrm{sub}} = 
V_{\mathrm{sub}}(\theta,h).
$$

The equilibrium equation is therefore rewritten as

$$
\rho_{\mathrm{water}}
V_{\mathrm{sub}}(\theta,h) = 
m.
$$

For every prescribed tilt angle $\theta$, the corresponding floating height $h$ must satisfy this equation. Since no analytical solution is generally available, the value of $h$ will be obtained numerically in MATLAB.

Once the floating equilibrium has been established, the submerged region can be determined, allowing the submerged volume, center of buoyancy, and restoring moment to be computed in the following sections.
